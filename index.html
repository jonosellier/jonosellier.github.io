Act-A-Fool

Game - JavaScript-based Progressive Web App, and Cordova-powered Android App

This was my very first attempt at using JavaScript to actually create a game for real people to use. It was created as part of an Independant Study at USF where you write a proposal on what you want to learn and how you plan on learning it. My proposal was based on cross-platform mobile development, a continued area of interst to me. My original intent was to make a Cards Agains Humanity app but the idea of manually rewriting every card by hand was dauning to me and as this was a 10-week course where I felt that I needed to provide meaningful progress every week, I decided that was too large a side-task that was not meeting the goals of my independant study. I later found out about JSON Againt Humanity by Chris Hallberg but I took a different route already.

The game was to charades as CAH was to mad-libs: a more modern, current twist on the classic game. It is meant to be played on a single phone/tablet where players take turns being given a card with a category and a person/thing/idea to pretend to be. No talking. Just acting (a fool). Points are added up and someone wins, fairly standard. Add in a twist of being able to make your own cards and cetegories so I didn't have to and we have a decent party game.

Due to limitations set by Cordova, the underlying web-app had to also be a single-page app. I am well aware of Angular, React, etc. but I set out to learn how to update the DOM myself anyways. I also made an effort to write all the code, markup, css, etc. myself and I ended up with minimal use of libraries and tools. This was primarily a learning experience for me and I hope that this is taken into context when you ask why I didn't use <library>

Navigate-A-Bull

Unnoficial Campus Navigation Service - Progressive Web App

In case you were curious, no, not all my projects are worded as Verb-a-Noun. The name comes from the long-standing tradition that USF must make all of its services some bull-based pun.

Nav-a-Bull is an app that solves an issue, USF is large and buildings are known as one thing on a map but another to students. New students struggle to find their classes every first week of class and my app, co-developed with Kelly Johnson and Philip Chang seeks to solve this.

Essentially what it comes down to is a skin over Google Maps (however it was intentionally designed to be able to be switched to any other mapping application if needed) that provided a list of places at USF that allow students to quickly see how to get to a given place without trying to find it on Google/Apple/Bing Maps. The entire idea was get in, get directions, get out. This app was designed to be quick to use and, going against what social media wants from us, minimize the time spent on the app.

To that end, we decided that installing an app was too much time spent trying to get directions so a PWA that could be linked via QR code on a physical map that is already on campus seemed like the lowest hurdle to use. That fact that it also satisfied my desire to make everything cross-platform under one codebase was quite pleasing too.
